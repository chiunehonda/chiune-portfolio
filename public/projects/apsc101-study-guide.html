Safety & Hand Tools

Module 5 · Week 1 — PPE, workspace protocols, and studio tools

Testable PPE & Safety Protocols

▼
Three mandatory PPE items: Safety glasses, long pants, and close-toed shoes. Missing any = may be asked to leave.

Safety glasses must be worn at all times in the studio, whether or not tools are in use. Prescription glasses are NOT safety glasses — you must wear safety glasses over them.

Injury Protocol

Minor injury: Notify studio instructor, get a bandage if needed.

Serious injury: (1) Stay with injured person, (2) Notify instructor immediately, (3) One person calls 911 and stays on the line, (4) Send a separate person outside to meet paramedics and guide them in.

The person on the phone with 911 must stay in the studio room with the injured person — do NOT be the one who goes outside.

Core Hand Tools

▼
Aviation Snips
Cut sheet metal along curved or straight lines. Different colors for different cut directions (left, right, straight).
Seamar
Folds sheet metal along a straight line to create bends and flanges.
Nibbler
Punches small notches to cut curves and complex shapes in sheet metal.
Hole Punch
Creates clean circular holes in sheet metal for rivets or fasteners.
Pop Riveter
Joins two pieces of sheet metal together using pop rivets (permanent mechanical fasteners).

Servo Motors & Forces

Module 5 · Weeks 1-2 — Operating principles, torque, and mechanical advantage

Core Servo Motor Components & Operation

▼

A servo motor moves to a commanded position (0°–180°), unlike a DC motor which rotates continuously.

Four Components

❶ DC Motor
Provides the rotational power
❷ Gears
Reduce speed and increase torque from the DC motor
❸ Output Position Sensor
Measures the current shaft angle
❹ Electronic Control System
Compares commanded position (from Arduino) to actual position and adjusts the motor current accordingly
This is a feedback control loop: Input (commanded angle) → Controller → Motor → Gears → Output shaft → Sensor feeds back to controller.

Practical Limitations

Limited rotation range (0–180°), limited torque output, limited speed, physical size and weight constraints. These directly affect retrieval system design.

Force & Torque Principles

Torque = Force × Distance. Attaching the cable further from the pivot increases the moment arm and thus the torque. Mounting the winch higher on the mast increases the effective pulling angle. Longer servo arm wires = greater leverage but less clamping force at the tips.

When the cable/wire connecting the servo arm to the claw is longer, the holding force at the grip point decreases because the moment arm on the object side increases.

▼
Risk = Severity × Likelihood

Severity: Maximum harm, consequences, or damage that could result.
Likelihood: Probability of the incident or event occurring.

▼
Preventable
Within the team's ability to minimize and control
Strategic
Knowingly accepted with hope of greater reward/payoff
External
Outside the team's control, but must still be considered

▼
TypeDescription

Key Concept Risk Matrix (5×5)

▼

Low (1-4): No action required. Medium (5-9): Monitor, some action. High (10-16): Implement risk reduction. Extreme (20-25): Unacceptable — must stop or address immediately.

▼
Avoid
Remove the risk entirely by changing what you're doing
Mitigate

Engineering Drawings

Module 5 · Week 3 — Isometric, orthographic, third angle projection, dimensioning

Testable Projection Types

▼
Perspective
Lines converge to show depth (realistic, like a photo)
Isometric
Three equal axes; parallel lines stay parallel; verticals stay vertical; horizontals rise at 30°; circles → ellipses
Principal/Orthographic
View directly along an axis; shows true shape of one face
Cabinet
Principal view with depth lines at an angle (least realistic)
Isometric = best for conceptual design (easy to understand shape). Orthographic = best for dimensioned/manufacturing drawings (reveals hidden features).

Testable Third Angle Projection

▼

Third angle projection is the North American standard. Views are arranged so:

• Top view is placed above the front view
• Right end view is placed to the right of the front view
• Left end view would go to the left

Front and top views align vertically. Front and end views align horizontally.

Think of it as "unfolding a box" — each face shows what you'd see looking at that side of the object. The standard 3-view drawing uses Front + Top + Right End.

Testable Dimensioning Guidelines

▼
  • Place dimensions outside the part views when possible
  • Extension lines come close to but don't touch the part
  • Extension lines from different dimensions may cross
  • Place dimensions between views when they apply to both
  • Dimension visible features, not hidden features when possible
  • No redundant dimensions — don't over-dimension (causes confusion and errors)
  • Choose which dimensions to show based on which are most critical for manufacturing
  • ⌀ symbol indicates a diameter dimension
  • Locate holes/circles by dimensioning their center position
Never place the same dimension on multiple views. If overall length = 96, notch = 35, and remaining = 61, you must eliminate one of the three (choose based on manufacturing importance).

Title Block Information

Drawing name, number, revision, scale, who prepared/checked it, units and precision, and the third angle projection symbol.

Copper Mining Overview

Module 6 · Week 5 — Mining lifecycle, open-pit vs underground, tailings

Testable Key Mining Terminology

▼
Minerals
Naturally occurring substances we extract through mining (not from plants/animals)
Ore
Rock containing minerals of interest, plus valueless material called gangue
Waste Rock
Rock surrounding ore that must be removed and stored to access the ore
Tailings
What remains after mineral extraction from ore during processing — a slurry of water and finely crushed gangue
Concentrate
Product of processing; typically 20–30% copper content

Testable Mine Development Cycle

▼
StageDurationKey Activities
Exploration8–10+ yearsLocate minerals, environmental assessments, Indigenous consultation
Assessments & ConsultationsOngoingRequired approvals; cannot proceed without them
Construction1–3 yearsBuild mine, processing plant, infrastructure, worker housing
Operation10–30 yearsActive mining and processing
Closure1–2 yearsRemove plant and equipment, begin land restoration
Rehabilitation & Monitoring1–2 yrs + 5+ yrs monitoringRestore environment to near-original state

Testable Open-Pit vs Underground Mining

▼
Open-Pit Mining ✓

Good for near-surface deposits. Faster extraction with bigger equipment. Cost-effective for low-grade deposits.

Drawbacks ✗

Large amounts of waste rock. Significant landform changes. May not be feasible in environmentally/culturally sensitive sites.

Underground Mining ✓

Good for deep deposits. Significantly less waste rock. Lower environmental footprint.

Drawbacks ✗

More expensive. Additional safety concerns (stability, ventilation, radiation). Slower due to less room and smaller equipment.

Tailings Management

Tailings dam/impoundment: Most common; slurry added continuously; water and solids separate over time. Must withstand extreme weather, earthquakes, climate change.

Dry stacking: Remove most water before storage; stored like waste rock. Impermeable liners protect against leaching.

Dewatering: Removing water from tailings. Backfilling: Returning processed material into underground mine voids for stability and waste reduction.

Sustainability & Systems Thinking

Module 6 · Week 5 — Three pillars, systems thinking principles, life cycle thinking

Testable Five Systems Thinking Principles

▼
  1. Consider the whole, not just the parts
  2. Consider connections and interdependence, not independent elements
  3. Look for circular relationships and feedback, not linear/deterministic views
  4. Synthesize — bring ideas together, don't just analyze parts
  5. Adopt multidisciplinary thinking, not siloed approaches

Three Types of Scales

Spatial
Physical extent — from chemical composition to regional impacts
Temporal
Time horizons — now, near future, generations to come
Organizational
Decision levels — project site, community, provincial/federal

Key Concept Life Cycle Thinking

▼

Stages (general product): Natural Resources → Extraction → Design & Production → Packaging & Distribution → Use & Maintenance → End of Life

End of life options (best to worst): Reuse → Recycling → Recovery → Incineration/Landfilling

For a mine, the appropriate life cycle is: Exploration → Development → Operation → Closure

Sustainability is a property of a SYSTEM, not an object. To develop sustainable solutions, you must look at entire systems through all life cycle stages using both systems thinking and life cycle thinking.

Key Concept Nested Sustainability Model

▼

The nested model shows: Economy ⊂ Society ⊂ Environment. The economy depends on society, and both depend on the environment. Without the ecosystem, the human world does not exist.

This contrasts with the Venn diagram model (three overlapping circles) — both are valid ways to represent the three dimensions of sustainability (economic, social, environmental).

Causal Loop Diagrams (CLDs)

Module 6 · Week 5-6 — Building and analyzing CLDs for complex systems

Testable CLD Fundamentals

▼

A CLD is a virtual prototype that maps relationships, dependencies, and feedback loops in a complex system.

Link Types

+ (positive link)
Variables change in the same direction. If A increases, B increases (and vice versa).
- (negative link)
Variables change in opposite directions. If A increases, B decreases (and vice versa).

Loop Types

Reinforcing loop (R)
Contains an even number of negative links (or all positive). Amplifies changes — can produce growth or collapse.
Balancing loop (B)
Contains an odd number of negative links. Seeks equilibrium — counteracts changes.
A CLD is NOT a calculation tool. It does not compute numerical outputs. It helps you understand qualitative relationships — which variables affect which, and whether the overall system amplifies or dampens changes.

Resilience

Module 6 · Week 6 — Resilient design principles for complex systems

Testable Resilience Definition & Principles

▼
Resilience = the capacity to adapt to changing conditions and to maintain or regain functionality and vitality in the face of stress or disturbance. Performance may be different from before (e.g., "run flat" tire — you can keep driving but slower).

Six Principles for Resilient Design

PrincipleMeaning
Function across scalesWork now and long-term, in different regions
Anticipate disturbancesConsider possible disturbances and changes during design
Build in redundancy & diversityDon't rely on a single approach; have backups
Be simple, passive, flexibleFunction without constant monitoring or input
Use locally available resourcesDon't rely on distant inputs; use renewable/local resources
Support community & social equityStrong communities cope better with stresses

Example: A grid road pattern is more resilient than a branching pattern because if one road is blocked, alternative routes exist. But grid patterns cost more to build and maintain.

LCA & SLCA

Module 6 · Weeks 6-7 — Life cycle assessment methods, functional units, sunk costs

Testable LCA: Four ISO Steps

▼
  1. Goal Definition & Scoping: Define system boundary, inputs (materials, energy), outputs (emissions, waste)
  2. Inventory Analysis: Track all material and energy flows into/out of the system (detailed, time-consuming)
  3. Impact Assessment: Examine environmental impacts using established metrics (resources, waste, land, water, air)
  4. Interpretation: Identify which stages/components contribute most; use to improve designs

Steps are iterative — results from later stages may cause revisiting earlier steps.

Testable Functional Units

▼

A functional unit is a reference quantity used to measure and normalize performance of alternatives for fair comparison.

Example: Comparing lightbulbs → use "40 million lumen-hours of light" (not "1 bulb" since they last different times).

Example: Comparing grocery bags → use capacity × uses (not "1 bag" since reusable bags are used many times).

Example: Car vs bus → use "person-km" (accounts for different passenger counts).

The functional unit must measure the function (useful output) fairly across alternatives. There is no single correct functional unit — it depends on the problem.

Testable SLCA vs LCA

▼
Full LCA

Quantitative, detailed, months to complete. Best for existing/mature products. Requires precise system knowledge.

Streamlined LCA (SLCA)

Qualitative, fast (hours/days), inexpensive. Best early in design. Captures ~80% of environmental issues from a full LCA.

SLCA Rating Scale

0 = Very Poor, 1 = Poor, 2 = Fair, 3 = Good, 4 = Very Good. Ratings are summed for an Environmentally Responsible Product Rating (RERP).

LCA Terminology

LCA
Life Cycle Assessment (typically environmental focus)
ELCA
Economic Life Cycle Assessment (sometimes used for "environmental")
S-LCA
Social Life Cycle Assessment
SLCA
Streamlined Life Cycle Assessment (no dash)

Testable Sunk Costs

▼
Sunk cost = a cost that has already been incurred and can't be recovered. In decision-making, sunk costs should be ignored.

Example: You bought a $1000 e-bike. Comparing trip costs: e-bike electricity ($0.50) vs Uber ($20). The $1000 purchase is a sunk cost — don't factor it in.

In Module 6: Exploration costs are sunk costs — they're the same regardless of which deposit is chosen. Don't include them in SLCAs or WDMs for comparing deposits.

Module 6 · Week 7 — Engagement continuum, social license, duty to consult

▼

InformProvide balanced information outwardNewsletters, websites, open houses, presentations
ConsultObtain and consider inputSurveys, townhalls, focus groups, interviews

Key Concept Social License & UNDRIP

▼

Social License to Operate: Local communities, stakeholders, and rights holders not only accept but actively support the company's activities.

UNDRIP: United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples. BC passed this into law (DRIPA) in November 2019.

Duty to Consult: The provincial (sometimes federal) government has a legal duty to consult with Indigenous rights holders. This can be delegated to the mining company ("proponent"), which then must: (1) provide information early, (2) identify and discuss potential impacts, (3) consider modifications to avoid/mitigate impacts, (4) document and report to the Province.

PlayPump lesson: Even well-intentioned engineering solutions fail without proper community engagement. The PlayPump was 4× more expensive and 4× less efficient than conventional pumps, and failed because it didn't adequately consider the community's actual needs and usage patterns.

• Letterhead only on first page; blank paper for subsequent pages.
• "Re:" line for reference numbers (bolded).

Satisfaction Curves

Module 7 · Week 8 — Constructing and interpreting satisfaction curves

Testable Satisfaction Curve Construction

▼

X-axis: Measurable, quantifiable attribute. Y-axis: Satisfaction (0% = barely acceptable, 100% = fully satisfied).

Three Key Points for Construction

1. Barely acceptable performance → Satisfaction ≈ 0%
2. What stakeholders expect → middle satisfaction (not necessarily 50%)
3. Everything they want → Satisfaction = 100%

Common Curve Shapes

S-curve (most common) — steep in the middle where stakeholders most notice differences. Linear — simple but usually not the best representation. Reverse S-curve — when lower values are better (e.g., cost). Peak in middle — optimal value exists. Discrete — categorical attributes. Step function — sharp drops near threshold values.

"All other things being equal": Each satisfaction curve considers only ONE attribute. Don't factor in trade-offs with other attributes (e.g., don't curve battery life satisfaction downward because bigger batteries are heavier — weight has its own curve).

Requirements as Thresholds

If there's a minimum acceptable value (requirement), any solution below it is screened out. Satisfaction = 0% at the threshold. Satisfaction plateaus at 100% once the maximum useful value is reached.

Satisfaction Formula Used in Module 7

S(x) = ½(1 − cos((x − x_min)/(x_max − x_min) × π))

For attributes where lower is better (like maintenance):

S(x) = ½(1 + cos((x − x_min)/(x_max − x_min) × π))

Fluid Mechanics

Module 7 · Weeks 9-11 — Pressure, flow, elevation, friction, and losses

Formula Hydrostatic Pressure

▼
p = ρgh
p
Pressure (Pa) — lowercase p for pressure
ρ
Water density ≈ 1000 kg/m³
g
Gravitational acceleration ≈ 9.81 m/s²
h
Height of water column above the point (m)
Pressure increases linearly with depth. We use atmospheric pressure as our reference (zero). Uppercase P is reserved for Power.

Key insight: Tank diameter D does NOT affect outflow rate — as D increases, weight increases but is divided over a larger area, so pressure (and thus flow) stays the same.

Formula Flow Rate and Velocity

▼
Q = v × A
Q
Volume flow rate (m³/s or L/min)
v
Flow velocity (m/s)
A
Cross-sectional area of pipe/nozzle (m²)

Flow moves from high pressure to low pressure. The pressure-velocity relationship:

ρv²/2 = p

Continuity (Conservation of Mass)

Water is incompressible → Q is the same everywhere in a pipe. If the nozzle is smaller than the pipe:

v × A = v_out × A_out

Smaller nozzle → faster exit velocity, but same volume flow rate.

Formula Pressure Losses (Three Types)

▼

1. Elevation Change

p_elevation = ρg(h₁ − h₂)

Raising water from h₁ to h₂ costs pressure. When outlet elevation = water surface, no flow (no pressure difference).

2. Pipe Friction

p_friction = f × (L/D) × (ρv²/2)
f
Friction factor (dimensionless)
L
Pipe length (m)
D
Pipe inner diameter (m)

Longer pipe → more friction → less flow. Like a long garden hose vs short one.

3. Restrictions & Fittings

p_restrictions = K × (ρv²/2)
K
Loss coefficient (dimensionless) — from elbows, valves, tees, unions

A valve works by varying K: slightly closed → small K → reduced flow. Fully closed → K = ∞ → no flow.

4. Filter Resistance

p_filter = C_f × v

C_f is the filter resistance coefficient. Note: filter losses are proportional to v (not v²).

Formula Complete System Equation

▼
ρv²/2 = p₁ − ρg(h₁−h₂) − f(L/D)(ρv²/2) − K(ρv²/2) − C_f·v

Or equivalently, the pump must supply:

p_pump = ρg·h_storage + f(L/D)(ρv²/2) + K(ρv²/2) + C_f·v

Each term on the right represents:

TermPhysical Meaning
ρg·h_storagePressure to lift water to storage elevation
f(L/D)(ρv²/2)Friction losses in pipe
K(ρv²/2)Losses from fittings/restrictions
C_f·vFilter resistance

RWH System Design

Module 7 · Weeks 9-13 — System components, pump and system curves, operating point

Core System Components

▼

Full system flow: Rain → Catchment area (roof) → Collection tank → Pump → Chemical treatment (ozone/chlorine) → Filter → Storage tank (elevated) → On-demand supply → Filter (alternative location) → UV purifier → House

Key Components

Catchment
Roof area that collects rainwater
Collection tank
Stores rainwater before pumping
Pump
Moves water from collection to elevated storage
Chemical treatment
Ozone or chlorine disinfection
Filtration
200μm (large particles), 5μm (silt), 1μm (pathogens). Required: 1μm filter + UV purifier.
UV purifier
Kills remaining pathogens after filtration
Storage tank
Elevated tank providing gravity-fed on-demand flow

Testable Pump Curve vs System Curve

▼

Pump curve: Shows pressure a pump can deliver vs flow rate. Higher Q → lower pressure. Curves downward.

System curve: Shows pressure required by the system vs flow rate. Higher Q → more losses → higher pressure needed. Curves upward. Y-intercept = ρg·h_storage.

Operating point = intersection of pump curve and system curve. This determines the actual Q and p at which the system runs. You don't choose Q — the system determines it.

Changing the Operating Point

• Lower storage elevation → system curve shifts down → higher Q
• Remove filters → system curve less steep → higher Q
• Shorter pipe / closer tank → less friction → higher Q
• Better/bigger pump → pump curve shifts up → higher Q

Trade-off: Higher storage elevation improves on-demand flow to the house, but makes the pump work harder (lower Q from pump to storage).

Testable On-Demand Flow (Storage → House)

▼

No pump on this line — driving pressure comes from elevated storage tank:

ρg·h_storage = f(L/D)(ρv²/2) + K(ρv²/2) + C_f·v

This is a quadratic equation in v. Rearranging: Av² + Bv + C = 0, then solve with the quadratic formula. Take the positive solution.

On the graph: the "pump" curve is a horizontal line at ρg·h_storage (constant pressure). The system curve starts at origin (no elevation to overcome on supply side). Operating point is where they intersect.

Energy & Pump Efficiency

Module 7 · Week 10 — Electrical energy, pump efficiency, diesel/solar calculations

Formula Energy Calculations

▼

Energy to Move Water

E_water = p_pump × V_water

Where V_water is the volume of water moved (m³) and p_pump is pump pressure (Pa). Result in Joules.

Electrical Energy (with efficiency)

E_elec = E_water / η

Where η is pump efficiency (0 < η ≤ 1). η varies with flow rate — not constant for real pumps.

Best Efficiency Point (BEP)

The flow rate where η is maximum. Operating far from BEP wastes energy. Pump curves often show both p(Q) and η(Q).

Power

P = E / t

Power (Watts) = Energy (Joules) / Time (seconds).

Filters & Maintenance

Module 7 · Weeks 11-12 — Filter coefficients, filter life, maintenance modeling

Testable Filter System Details

▼

Three Filter Types

FilterSizeRemovesRelative C_f
Bag filter200 μmSand, dust, pollenLowest
Cartridge5 μmSilt, suspended solids~20× higher
Cartridge1 μmFine particles, some pathogens~2× higher than 5μm
Required: 1μm filter + UV purifier on the supply line. 200μm and 5μm are optional but extend the life of downstream filters.

Filter Life & Maintenance

Filter life depends on which pre-filters are upstream. Without pre-filters, finer filters foul faster → more frequent replacement → more maintenance events → lower satisfaction.

Trade-off: More filters = better water quality, longer individual filter life, but higher initial cost and lower flow rate (higher C_f).

Scale Model Testing

C_f,exp is measured experimentally. Real filter C_f values are scaled from the experiment:

C_f (200μm) = 0.00417 × C_f,exp
C_f (5μm) = 0.0833 × C_f,exp
C_f (1μm) = 0.167 × C_f,exp

RWH Risk & Curve Fitting

Module 7 · Weeks 10-11 — Health/environmental risk, R², trendlines

Testable Health & Environmental Risk Matrix

▼

Risk Likelihood Formula

Risk Likelihood = 4 − 0.5 × ln(# days between exposures)

Where # days is the period between risk exposures. Daily = 4, Weekly ≈ 3, Monthly ≈ 2, Yearly ≈ 1.

Overall Risk Exposure

E = sum of all individual risk scores (severity × likelihood for each health and environmental risk from each component: diesel, chlorine/ozone, batteries, etc.)

Risk Satisfaction

S(E) = ½(1 + cos((E − E_min)/(E_max − E_min) × π))

Where E_min and E_max come from the component selection guide.

Testable Curve Fitting & R²

▼

R² (coefficient of determination) measures how well data matches the best-fit curve. Range: 0 to 1.

R² = 1 means y can be perfectly predicted from x. R² = 0 means no relationship.

Key Points

• Choose the curve type based on the physical phenomenon (not just highest R²)
• A 2nd-order polynomial with 3 data points always gives R² = 1 (same # of coefficients as points)
• When fitting a line to 2 points, it's trivial (exact solution)

Excel bug: When you force a linear trendline to a non-zero y-intercept, Excel shows the wrong R² value (same as the unforced version). Don't trust R² in this case. Google Sheets doesn't support forced intercepts for trendlines at all.
